The effects of dispersion.

A wave is 'dispersive' if the phase velocity of the wave is dependent on the wavelength (or equivalently, the wavenumber) of the wave. We've seen a number of 'non-dispersive' waves so far, and one 'dispersive' wave (a free surface gravity wave).

Shallow water waves & elastic strings are non-dispersive:

In non-dispersive waves the phase speed is a constant. Here we show an initial condition (a Gaussian hump) evolving in a non-dispersive system. Note the lack of signal distortion as time evolves as all Fourier components propagate at the same speed.

Weak dispersion:

Water waves are only non-dispersive in the LIMIT of shallow water (i.e., as the wavelength of the wave tends to infinity, or fluid depth tends to zero). In practice, for a given wavelength and fluid depth (such that the ratio of fluid depth over wavelength is small) the system will only be non-dispersive to leading order. Any correction terms will, in general, cause dispersion, albeit a 'weak' dispersion. Here is the same problem, but with a small correction that we may associate with the problem being 'almost' a shallow water wave problem. Note that there is some slight signal distortion.

Free surface gravity waves are dispersive:

This case is a (infinite depth) free surface gravity wave, which we've seen is dispersive in the lecture notes. We've shown that long waves propagate faster than short waves. You can see this as time evolves -- note that the initial signal is the same shape as the non-dispersive case above. The dependence of the phase speed on the wavelength causes the signal to distort and the evolution is considerably more complicated.
