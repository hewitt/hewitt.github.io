Group velocity 2

Evolution of a single group.

See section 3.10 in the lecture notes.

The simplistic argument of just adding two waves together also applies to a narrow (continuous) band of wave frequencies. Here, instead of 2 discrete waves, we look at the evolution of just such a narrow band for a free surface gravity wave. The animations are the SAME wave, only the frame of reference changes (and its always stationary or in uniform translation).

In a stationary frame of reference:

Here we see a single group evolving with time. The wave is viewed from a stationary frame of reference. Note that the group as a whole is moving. The individual wave crests are propagating more quickly than the group in this case, disappearing at the 'front' and appearing at the 'back'.

In a frame of reference that moves with the phase speed:

Here we see a single group evolving with time. The wave is viewed from a uniformly translating frame of reference, moving with the phase speed. Because our reference frame is moving with the phase speed, the individual crests/troughs appear stationary. However, because in this case the group velocity is less than the phase speed the group propagates to the left.

In a frame of reference that moves with the group velocity:

Here we see a single group evolving with time. The wave is viewed from a uniformly translating frame of reference, moving with the group velocity. Because our reference frame is moving with the group, the maximum of the envelope is stationary. However, because in this case the group velocity is less than the phase speed the we see the individual crests and troughs propagating to the right. They disappear at the 'front' and appear at the 'back' of the group.
